Overview

The Problem

University libraries have evolved into essential collaborative spaces. At Iowa State University, the Parks Library study rooms are among the most heavily used spaces on campus. However, the current booking system — built on Springshare's LibCal platform — creates significant usability friction for students trying to reserve a room.

Our user research consistently documented four critical pain points in the existing interface:

Pain Point 1 Fragmented navigation caused students to lose their selected options every time they clicked into a room's detail page.
Pain Point 2 A dense spreadsheet-style time grid confused users — clicking one 30-minute slot automatically selected a 60-minute block without clear feedback.
Pain Point 3 Equipment booking was routed through a completely separate tab, leaving users disoriented and unsure whether they were booking a room or equipment.
Pain Point 4 There was no built-in mechanism for sharing reservations with group members, so students resorted to sending screenshots via text message.

Research

Understanding the Users

We conducted semi-structured interviews with five representative users to identify usability problems and understand user mental models. From this research, we developed three personas representing the range of user needs:

Becca — The Experienced Planner

Books regularly for group meetings. Has developed workarounds for system limitations. Needs a unified flow for comparing rooms and booking equipment.

David — The Efficiency Seeker

Just wants a space and chooses the first available option. Needs a streamlined path with minimal clicks and decision points.

Maya — The Novice User

Books primarily on mobile. Needs visual clarity and intuitive spatial orientation.

"I could see how the room looks without clicking on the room. The current way doesn't come naturally." — Participant P1

Design

The Redesign

Following the Double Diamond design process, we moved from paper prototypes through heuristic evaluation to a high-fidelity Figma prototype. The redesign introduced four key improvements:

1. Visual Room Browsing and Comparison

Rooms are displayed as visual cards with photos, capacity, feature tags, and availability indicators. When a user clicks a card, the detail panel opens on the right side while the room list stays visible, eliminating the loss-of-state problem entirely.

Room browsing with filter sidebar and card layout
Persistent date banner and calendar selector

2. Persistent Date and Visual Timeline

A prominent date banner persists across every screen in the booking flow. A drag-to-select visual timeline replaces the confusing spreadsheet grid, making duration selection explicit and immediately editable.

3. Unified Equipment Booking

Equipment selection is integrated directly into the booking flow as the next step after time selection. Users maintain a continuous mental model of their reservation without the disorientation of switching between tabs or systems.

Integrated equipment booking with room summary panel
Confirmation page with built-in group sharing

4. Quick Book for Immediate Needs

A novel Quick Book feature allows students already at the library to find and reserve an available room in just three interactions, compared to the current system's minimum of eight to ten steps. The flow asks two simple questions — group size and duration — and surfaces the nearest matching room with a floor map for wayfinding.

Evaluation

User Study Results

We conducted a controlled within-subjects study with 9 Iowa State University students (8 graduate, 1 undergraduate), comparing the redesigned Figma prototype against the current LibCal system. Participants completed three core booking tasks on both interfaces, with order counterbalanced to control for learning effects. Each session lasted approximately 50 minutes and included NASA-TLX workload surveys, a Single Ease Question after each task, and a final System Usability Scale (SUS) evaluation of the redesign.

Task Completion Time

Average task completion time decreased from 120 seconds on the current system to 78 seconds on the redesign — a reduction of roughly 35% across all three tasks. The improvement was most dramatic on Task 3 (booking equipment), where one participant spent nearly 550 seconds on the current system before giving up, compared to near-zero errors and consistently short completion times on the redesign. Box plot analysis showed the redesigned interface not only performed faster on average, but also had significantly tighter quartile ranges, indicating a more consistent and predictable experience for all users.

Errors and Backtracks

Error rates dropped from an average of 4.08 errors per task on the current system to just 1.1 on the redesign (p<0.001). The most striking finding was Task 3 on the redesigned interface: only one participant made any errors at all, and the rest completed the equipment booking step without a single mistake — a direct result of unifying the equipment flow into the same page as the room booking. Backtracks, defined as recovery actions after a wrong turn, also fell sharply, reaching zero for Task 3 on the redesign compared to the high backtrack rates observed on the current system across all tasks.

Task Success Rate

On the current system, one participant failed to complete Tasks 1 and 2, and over half of all participants failed to complete Task 3 — the equipment booking step — entirely. On the redesign, there were zero task failures across all participants and all tasks. This finding alone speaks to how fundamentally the current equipment booking flow was broken for users.

Cognitive Workload (NASA-TLX)

NASA-TLX scores showed a significant decrease across five of the six subscales on the redesigned interface: mental demand, physical demand, temporal demand, effort, and frustration all decreased substantially. Perceived performance — how successfully participants felt they accomplished the task — significantly increased. Overall workload dropped by roughly half across all dimensions, confirming that the redesign reduced not just the time and errors, but the cognitive burden users experienced throughout the booking process.

System Usability Scale (SUS)

The redesign received a mean SUS score of 84.2 out of 100. Only one participant scored it below the industry average of 68 (scoring 62), and only one other scored it below 70 (at 66). Every other participant rated usability at 76 or higher. A score above 80 is considered excellent and positions a product in the top tier of usability benchmarks — the redesign comfortably crossed this threshold.

Qualitative Feedback

All nine participants stated they would prefer to use the redesigned interface in the future. Their reasons centered on three themes: fewer steps to complete the same task, clearer visual feedback at each stage, and a more logical flow between booking, equipment, and confirmation. Several participants specifically noted the persistent date banner and the side-panel room comparison as features that reduced their frustration compared to the current system.

100% of participants preferred the redesigned interface, citing "less steps," "easier to understand," and "good feedback" as their primary reasons. The redesign fully supported all three research hypotheses: faster completion time (H1), fewer errors (H2), and lower cognitive workload with high usability (H3).

Reflection

What I Learned

This project reinforced the value of early requirements gathering through behavioral observation rather than relying solely on self-reported preferences. The gap between what users said they needed and what they actually struggled with during testing was significant — and only visible through watching real interactions.

The project also demonstrated that targeted, user-centered redesign of an existing institutional interface can produce meaningful, measurable improvements without replacing the underlying system — a model that could be replicated across many university platforms.

Future Directions

Priority areas for future investigation include mobile interface testing (a significant portion of users book on phones), longitudinal evaluation to assess whether efficiency gains persist over time, and field deployment with real room availability data to test performance under actual booking constraints.
